Mechanics (Core 1)

Posted on Jun 30, 2021

Vectors

Notations

  • the basis is the set of unit vectors from which all others are built
  • perpendicular axes normally have basis vectors $\boldsymbol{\hat{i},\hat{j},\hat{k}}$
    • helpful to remember: i = 8 o’clock, j = 4 o’clock, k = 12 o’clock (standard)
    • this is right-handed axes (anti-clockwise)
  • $\boldsymbol{\hat{a}} = \frac{\boldsymbol{a}}{\left|a\right|}$

Components

  • $\theta$ taken from x-axis
    • $a_x = a\cos{\theta}$
    • $a_y = a\sin{\theta}$
    • $\tan{\theta} = \frac{a_y}{a_x}$
    • $\left|a\right| = \sqrt{a_x^2+a_y^2}$
  • 3D components
    • pretty much compliant with 2D and 1D examples, but the concept of an angle might be harder to visualise, we rely on the unit vectors as it’s a much easier system.

Position vectors

  • specifies position relative to a defined origin
  • not all quantities have position vectors
    • forces do not have origins
  • arbitrary $\boldsymbol{r}$ is a vector with $(x,y,z)$ components which can vary with time.
    • $\boldsymbol{r}(t)$ can describe a point P
    • one can perform standard calculus to find velocity/acceleration etc.

Vector algebra

  • associative
  • commutative
  • distributive
  • there is no such thing as division
  • scalar multiplication
  • products
    • dot
    • cross
  • magnitude and direction are fixed in space and do not depend on choice of coordinate system (absolute-space)
  • 3D directions
    1. normally taken as an angle projected off a plane
    2. direction cosines: components of the unit vectors
      • each component is projected onto their respective axis (the cosines of the magnitude) so we need to calculate 3 angles
  • rotated frames
    • (x,y,z) rotated by $\theta$
    • vectors have the same magnitudes with directions fixed in space described relative to frame
    • components are related by $\phi, \phi ‘$
      • $\phi ' = \phi - \theta$
      • using trigonometric angle additions
        • $a_x’=a_x\cos(\theta) + a_y\sin(\theta)$
        • $a_y'=a_y\cos(\theta) - a_x\sin(\theta)$
        • for basis vectors:
          • $\boldsymbol{i} = \boldsymbol{i'}\cos{\theta} - \boldsymbol{j'}\sin{\theta}$
          • $\boldsymbol{j} = \boldsymbol{i'}\sin{\theta} + \boldsymbol{j'}\cos{\theta}$
          • $\boldsymbol{i'} = \boldsymbol{i}\cos{\theta} + \boldsymbol{j}\sin{\theta}$
          • $\boldsymbol{j'} = - \boldsymbol{i}\sin{\theta} + \boldsymbol{j}\cos{\theta}$
  • polar coordinates: an easier system

Equations of motion

Constant acceleration

  • calculus in mechanics
    • the derivative of a quantity tells us the rate of change of the quantity
    • the integral of a quantity tells us the area under the function describing that quantity.
    • notations: $\ddot{s} = \dot{v} = a$
    • wrapping my head around signs
      • a -ve slope = derivative is -ve
    • classifying stationary points by inspection
      • maximum: +ve (approaching $x$) and -ve (receding $x$)
      • minimum: -ve (approaching x) and +ve (approaching x)
      • o/e inflection
  • 1D Newton’s laws (simple but important)
    • NII: sum of resultant forces acting on a body is the product of mass and resultant acceleration
      • assumption: constant acceleration $a(t) = a_0$
      • dummy variables: the sole purpose is to retain the final results with our main variables like $t$ or $v$.
  • equations of motion sans derivation
    • $v = v_0 + at$
    • $s = s_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2$
    • $v^2 = v_0^2 + 2as$
    • $s = \frac{1}{2}(v+v_0)t$

Varying acceleration

  • easy if acceleration is a linear function of time
    • Example: $a(t) = a_0 + a_1t$
      • $v$ found by integrating once
        • $v(t) = a_0t + \frac{1}{2!}a_1t^2$
      • $s$ found by integrating once more
        • $\frac{1}{2}a_0t^2 + \frac{1}{3!}a_1t^3$
  • if acceleration is a function of position, it might be possible to use chain rule
    • Using $a(s) = v\frac{dv}{ds}$
    • $a(s) = a_0 + a_1s$
      • $v$ found by integrating $a\cdot ds$ and $v\cdot dv$
        • $v = \pm \sqrt{2a_0s + \frac{1}{2}a_1s^2 + C}$
  • physical example 1 - SHM:
    • $-kx = ma$
      • eventually leads to $KE = -PE + $ const
    • $\ddot{x} = -\omega x$
    • boundary conditions:
      • $v = v_{max}, x = 0$ and the inverse also holds
      • this leads to max KE = max KE
  • physical example 2 - gravity well:
    • Newton’s law of gravitation: $F = -\frac{GMm}{r^2} = ma$
    • integrating the differentials $dr$ and $dv$ gives change in PE = change in KE
  • fundamental point: conservation of energy holds

Equations of motions (vectors)

Constant acceleration

  • all systems of equations can be rewritten in vector forms

Varying acceleration

  • example: $\boldsymbol{a} = a_0t\boldsymbol{\hat{i}}+a_0\boldsymbol{\hat{j}}$
    • integrate both sides would give velocity vector $\boldsymbol{v} = \left(\frac{a_0t^2}{2}+v_{0x}\right)\boldsymbol{\hat{i}} + \left(a_0t+v_{0y}\right)\boldsymbol{\hat{j}}$
    • repeat for position vector with positional constants
  • circular motion
    • polar coordinate system $\left(r,\theta\right)$
      • $\boldsymbol{r}(t) = r\cos{\omega t}\boldsymbol{\hat{i}} + r\sin{\omega t}\boldsymbol{\hat{j}}$
    • get something like SHM
    • velocity vector tangential to the circular motion
      • $\left|v\right| = r\omega$
    • acceleration always radial towards origin
      • $\left|a\right| = r\omega^2$

Newton’s first law

  • statement 1: force is needed to change motion, i.e. force is proportional to the rate of change of velocity.
    • it reveals nothing about absolute velocity
    • it is really about relativity :classical relativity:
      • but it does postulate an absolute space
  • statement 2: every body preserves its state of rest or uniform motion, unless acted on by an external force

Reference frames

  • inertial frames
    • a frame of reference associated with an inertial observer moving at constant velocity
    • a frame that is not accelerating
    • converting between inertial frames, A and B:
      • origin coincides at $t = 0$
      • origin of B in A: $x = vt$
        • point in B with coordinate $x'$
          • measured in A as $x = vt + x'$
          • general vector case $\boldsymbol{r} = \boldsymbol{v}t+\boldsymbol{r'}$
      • velocity
        • differentiate wrt time
          • $\boldsymbol{u = u' + v}$
          • object moving at $\boldsymbol{u'}$ in B, which is moving at $v$ wrt A, is moving at $\boldsymbol{u}$ in A.
      • acceleration
        • $\boldsymbol{\dot{u}}=\boldsymbol{ \dot{u'} }$
        • the action of forces is independent of inertial frames
      • momentum
        • $\boldsymbol{p = p'} + m\boldsymbol{v}$
    • all laws independent of frame of reference $s=uv$
    • higher dimensions
      • just use use vectors

Momentum and c.o.m. frame

  • one can derive conservation of momentum from NI.
    • in the absence of external forces, total momentum is conserved, i.e. constant in time
      • $\frac{d\boldsymbol{p_{tot}}}{dt}= 0$
  • in a system of N particles, the $\boldsymbol{p_{tot}}$ is the sum of the products of individual masses and velocities (all momenta). can write their velocities in terms of rate of change of position
    • what is the c.o.m.:
      • a point in a mass system where any force applied cannot exert moment or torque on the body or plane (no angular acceleration)
        • from this definition:
        • $\sum_{j=1}^{N} m_j\left(\boldsymbol{r_j}-\boldsymbol{r_{c.o.m.}}\right) = 0$
        • rationale: all particles exert a force of $m_j\boldsymbol{g}$, at the c.o.m. their total moments sum to zero, so we can just divide by $\boldsymbol{g}$
        • $\boldsymbol{r_{c.o.m.}} = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{j=1}^{N} m_j\boldsymbol{r_j}$
        • relating to momentum: $\boldsymbol{p_{tot}}=M\cdot\boldsymbol{v_{c.o.m.}}$
        • in words, the total momentum is the product of total mass and velocity of c.o.m.
          • advantages: ignore individual motion of particles and consider only the c.o.m.
      • it is the mean location of the distribution of mass
  • c.o.m. frame (zero momentum frame)
    • inertial frame chosen such that $\boldsymbol{v_{c.o.m.}}=0$
      • strategy: find the velocity of the c.o.m. of a system and transfer to that frame so that it is stationary
        • this frame will continue to be the zero-momentum provided there will not be any external forces

Newton’s second law and forces

  • statement:
    1. the rate of change of momentum of a body is proportional to the applied force, and takes place in the direction of that force.
      • if momentum is conserved, this implies that $\boldsymbol{F} = \frac{d\boldsymbol{p}}{dt} = 0$
      • this is inline with conservation of momentum derived from NI
      • forces lead to a change in momentumm, which can be a change in speed, direction (since velocity is a vector), or both (or mass).
  • impulse:
    • the force integrated over a given time interval
      • $\Delta \boldsymbol{N} = \int_{t_0}^{t_1} \boldsymbol{F} ,dt$
    • Impulse-momentum theorem: from NII, we can deduce that impulse is the change in velocity.
      • the duration of impulse is often short (10-500 ms), and the shape of $\boldsymbol{F}\left(t\right)$ is generally not important as we just want the integral.
        • we can say that since the duration is small, we can use an average force (that the force is constant).
        • thus $\Delta \boldsymbol{N} = \bar{F}\delta t$
  • contact forces versus force fields
    • act at a point on the body, generally due to contact with another part of the system
      • examples: friction, tension, steam pushing against an engine piston
      • on a macro level, these are referred to as contact forces even if on a micro level, these are defined as interactions within force fields.

Friction

  • empirically, friction is observed to be proportional to normal reaction force, with the constant of proportionality the coefficient $\mu$
  • friction acts to oppose the direction of motion
  • if the body is already moving, we consider kinetic friction, related as $\boldsymbol{f} = -\boldsymbol{\hat{v}}\mu_k\left|\boldsymbol{N}\right|$, which can also be written in scalar form
  • if the body is not moving, we consider static friction
    • friction is a limiting force, it opposes forces with magnitude big enough to prevent motion
    • the relation is $\boldsymbol{f}\leq\mu_sN$
    • when the driving force exceeds this, then the body will begin to slide, then it will continue in general
  • rolling friction
    • pulling through c.o.m., and the point of contact is a static point as there is no relative movement (no sliding)
    • movement comes from friction as a torque acting through c.o.m.
    • rolling resistance (coefficient of rolling friction) $\mu_r$
      • determines friction and energy loss when rolling

Drag

  • friction due to motion through a fluid, gas or liquid
    • opposes net motion like friction
    • amplitude of the friction is generally proportional to the speed of the motion raised to some power $n$.
    • constant of proportionality $k$ is the drag constant (not the drag coefficient!)
      • the unit of the drag constant: kg/m
  • the drag coefficient
    • normally $c_d = \frac{2k}{\rho A}$

Fields

  • gravitational field
    • $\boldsymbol{F_{12}}=\frac{Gm_1m_2}{r_{12}^2}\boldsymbol{\hat{r}_{12}}$
  • electric fields
    • $\boldsymbol{F} = q\boldsymbol{E}$
      • direction depends on sign of charge
  • magnetic fields
    • $\boldsymbol{F}=q\boldsymbol{v}\times\boldsymbol{B}$
  • Lorentz force
    • $\boldsymbol{F}=q\left(\boldsymbol{E} + \boldsymbol{v \times B}\right)$

Rocket equation

  • rate of change of mass non-zero
    • mass ejected at relative velocity $\boldsymbol{ v_{rel} }$
    • conserved quantities: momentum, total mass of system
  • relates the resultant thrust of the rocket depending on the relative velocity of the exhaust gass and the rate of change of mass
    • $m\frac{d\boldsymbol{v}}{dt}=\boldsymbol{v_{rel}}\frac{dm}{dt}$
    • sign sanity check:
      • we want thrust in the +ve direction
      • $v_{rel}$ is in the -ve direction, rate of change of mass is also negative since the rocket is losing mass
  • with the influence of gravity
    • the thrust has a negative term of $-m\boldsymbol{ g }$ (slightly inaccurate)
    • this gives velocity $\boldsymbol{v = v_0 + v_{rel}}\log_e\frac{m}{m_0}+\boldsymbol{g}t$

Newton’s third law

  • statement: to any action there is an equal and opposite reaction; in other words, the actions of two bodies on each other are always equal and always opposite in direction

Two-body problem

  • consider two bodies 1 and 2, then each exert a force on the other
  • from NII, we can find the acceleration of one body from their masses and the other body’s acceleration
    • since these are non-inertial frames, acceleration of body 1 in the frame of body 2 is $\boldsymbol{a_{{net}} = a_1 - a_2}$
    • this gives $\boldsymbol{a_{net}} = \left(\frac{m_2 + m_1}{m_1m_2}\right)\cdot m_1\boldsymbol{a_1}$ let the term in bracket be $\mu$ (otherwise known as the *reduced mass*)
      • in body 2, force it exerts on 1 is $\boldsymbol{F_{12}} = \mu\boldsymbol{a_{net}}$

Composite rigid bodies

  • a system of particles can be treated as one body when considering its c.o.m.
  • with N3, the c.o.m. motion is not affected because it only relates to the individual forces acting on each particle, not the net force
    • in a closed system, the rate of change of momentum is 0
    • in an open system, this will be equal to the external force, and thus $\boldsymbol{v_{com}}\neq 0$
      • this force acts at c.o.m, and cause acceleration of the total mass
      • an external force which does not act through the c.o.m may cause rotation about some axis
      • thus, the complete picture of the motion of a rigid body is
        1. motion of c.o.m
        2. rotation about c.o.m

Work

  • definition: when a force acts on a particle such that its point of action moves through a distance, we say work is done on the particle.
    • key equations:
      • $W = \int_{x=0}^{d} F\left(x\right) \cdot dx$
        • note that it is a dot product, meaning a force at an angle obeys the dot product
        • the general case would be a line integral where dr might vary as well
      • $P = Fv$
  • in different reference frames:
    • since KE will be different in 2 frames, statement relating to conservation of energy must be restricted to a single given reference frame

Potential energy

  • we can define a potential difference of a particle as the work done against the force
    • as defined by a path from A to B:
      • $\Delta V = - \int_{0}^{B} F\left(x\right) dx - - \int_{0}^{A} F\left(x\right) dx$
      • both points use the same reference point (this can be at the origin, or at infinity, it does not actually matter)
  • it is a scalar function
    • $F\left(x\right) = -\frac{dV\left(x\right)}{dx}$
    • or i.e., force is the negative gradient of the potential

Mechanical energy

  • energy is dissipated when work is done by friction or drag
  • the total energy which is the sum KE and PE minus energy dissipated, must be constant in time in a closed system to conserve energy
    • a closed system has no external forces
    • in the presence of external forces, the work done externally has to be factored in as it leads to changes in both KE & PE

Conservative forces

  • definition
    • if we can define a change in potential in terms of the work done against a force, and that the potential does not depend on the path taken, then that force is considered to be conservative
      • from this, we can deduce that the work done by a conservative force around a closed loop is zero
      • energy is conserved under the action of a conservative force
      • forces which lead to energy dissipation in the form of heat loss is non-conservative

Application: transfer to c.o.m. frame

  • since total momentum is always zero in the c.o.m. frame
    • 2 particles moving in 2D towards each other has a $\boldsymbol{v_{com}}$
      • $\boldsymbol{v_{com}} = \frac{m_1\boldsymbol{u_1}+m_2\boldsymbol{u_2}}{m_1+m_2}$ here
      • find $\boldsymbol{u'1} = \boldsymbol{u_1 - v{com}}$
      • and $\boldsymbol{u'2} = \boldsymbol{u_2 - v{com}}$
      • also final velocities, use this to find coefficient of restitution $e = \frac{\left|\boldsymbol{v_2-v_1}\right|}{\left|\boldsymbol{u_2-u_1}\right|}$
      • also ratio between total final KE and total initial KE is $e^2$ in this frame
