1. Introduction

Notes

1:2

  • Aim of this book:

2:1

  • Definition of realism:
    • Realism about a particular domain is the conjunction (x and y) of the theses:
      • (i) the existence thesis: there are facts or entities distinctive of that domain
      • (ii) the independence thesis: their existence and nature is in some important sense objective and mind-independent

2:2

  • Existence thesis (elaboration):
    • Caveat: involves quantification over entities and facts
      • Entities: referents of singular terms
      • Facts: aspects of the world represented by whole declarative sentences
      • Those who distinguished existence and independence thesis only quantified it over individuals
      • Q: why is the involvement of both facts and entities awkward?
      • Q: what does quantifying it over individuals mean?
    • Except: it is possible to be a realist about a domain without thinking there are any special entities distinctive of that domain
      • Example: What goes up must come down as a fact, but gravitational fields is not a realist entity
      • They are considered scientific realists, not realists about scientific entities
        • Other domains:
          Facts Objects
          scientific realism scientific entities
          realism about fiction fictional objects
          modal realism (wiki) possible worlds
          moral realism moral properties

3:1

  • For a domain to be objective and mind-independent, it must not be any of the following:
    • sense-data of phenomenalism (Russell’s table)
    • ideas of idealism (Berkeleyan subjective idealism: ideas exist only in mind, so is wholly mind-independent)
    • monads of Leibniz
    • phenomenal world of Kant (versus the noumenal)
    • verifiable world of the logical positivist (verificationism: meaning derived from verifiability)
  • Realist facts must be discovered, not constructed
    • Q: is there a language barrier somewhere? Can the world exist without any ideas/thoughts, and facts in their domains are present, but in a crude form that is then constructed into coherent language?
  • Entities must be detected, not constituted mentally

3:2

  • Contains quotes of what other philosopher defines realism to be

4:2

  • A diagram showing realism and anti-realism with respect to mind-independent and existence (or non-existence)
  • Third quadrant is empty, this is because the two theses are not independent of one another.
    • In order for an individual or fact to be dependent on our minds, it must exist; mind-dependence therefore guarantees existence
    • mind-dependence $\rightarrow$ existence

5:3

  • Some philosophers espouse that realism requires the following supplement:
    • The confidence thesis: Although it may in some circumstances be difficult, we are always capable of coming to know about the existence and nature of the domain we are realists about. That domain is epistemically accessible to us
      • This position directly opposes the sceptic, it is an optimistic alternative to scepticism

6:1

  • Smart, Papineau, Pettit, Thomasson, and others added:
    • The insecurity thesis: It is possible to be in ignorance of error about the domain we are realists about. To avoid such mistakes, one must make appropriate contact with the domain in question, and there is no guarantee that anyone will succeed in doing that.

6:2

  • These two supplementary positions are not contradictory, the insecurity thesis does aid scepticism but cannot pave way to scepticism without completely denying the confidence thesis.

6:3

  • It is controversial to introduce an epistemic component to realism
    • Q: What does this mean? Is an epistemic component involving our own knowledge being able to realise things? A: The epistemic account involves the supplementary theses
    • If we are Cartesians about ourselves (knowing our own existence incorrigibly and infallibly), according to the insecurity thesis, we would still undermine this.
    • Objects outside our light cone cannot be observed, but then according to the confidence thesis, this would undermine their reality.
    • Thus, canonical realism in this book does not include the supplementary theses (they are called supplementary for this exact reason).

7:1

  • Some also scrap existence and independence thesis for a semantic thesis
    • This involves linguistic philosophy
    • It provides an emphasis on truth and meaning Q: instead of mind and metaphysics?

7:2

  • Dummett attempted such re-characterisation
    • Distinctive statements are either true or false, independent of our means for knowing it.
    • Distinguishes realism and anti-realism with respect to truth
      • Realism: a proposition is true iff it corresponds to the facts. Correspondence theory of truth
      • Anti-realism: a theory that equates truth with warranted assertability, practical utility, or other.
  • This semantic take is controversial
    • Kuhn, Putnam, Dummett and Wright gave a semantic spin
    • Lewis, Devitt and Horwich deny any connection between the problem of realism and the problems of truth and meaning.
    • Q: Realism is a metaphysical position, while true and meaning is to do with epistemic or semantic?

Summary

This section introduces realism and touches on anti-realism by denoting what realism is not. It lays out the 2 main theses of realism; the existence and independence theses. Besides the two main theses, other philosophers also like the idea of an epistemic component of realism. However, this book will avoid including the two epistemic theses as part of the realist canonical because it leads to a lot of controversy.

16:1 2.2.1 Ockham’s Razor

  • There is a lot of disagreement over what exactly the principle of parsimony is and what it counsels us to do (despite being non-controversial)
  • This is not life guidance, it is guidance for our choices between competing theories.

16:2

  • Thus it favours anti-realism over realism.
    • Realism: Fs exist because our accepted theories are ontologically committed to Fs.
    • Anti-realism: There is an alternative theory that is not committed to Fs but as serviceable
      • committed to nothing not already precolor-keephue: trueostulated by the accepted theory.
      • Ockham’s razor: we should believe that there are no Fs.

17:1

      • Keeps down the number of different kinds of entity

    • Quantitative
      • Keeps down the number of instances of the kinds it posits.

18:2

    • Entities should not be multiplied beyond necessity

    • But there is a weaker alternative interpretation of the razor:
      • If there is no necessity to postulate an entity, we should suspend judgement about its existence (named the deletion rule)

  • Only the replacement rule supports an anti-realist position, since the deletion rule simply suspends judgement and does not posit anything interesting.

21:2 2.2.2 Reductionism and attitude problems

27:2 2.3: Alternatives to realism

  • This section concerns a taxonomy of anti-realism

27:3 & 27:4 & 28:1 2.3.1: Error theory

  • POSITION: top left
  • Claims that there are no Fs.
  • If we make truth-apt claims about Fs that advocates truth, we are guilty of systematic mistakes.
  • Most straightforward variety of anti-realism.
    • Cognitivism about Fs, but that statements and beliefs about Fs are all false
    • Deny (P1) of the Quinean argument.
      • We can make accepted theories about Fs without asserting Fs' existence.
      • Such beliefs are unjustified

28:2 28:3 29:1 2.3.2: Prefix fictionalism

  • POSITION: top left
    • There exists ‘pretence fictionalism’, which does not make use of any implicit premises but of a collective pretence.
  • General strategy for paragraphing away aspects of our accepted theories that have unwanted OCs.
  • Claims about Fs are truth-apt and true.
    • Also believed, asserted and assented to.
  • Unlike realists, do not take these claims at face value.
  • Taken as longer statements about the content of a theory.
  • Every claim committing the speaker to a domain of Fs contains an implicit prefix
    • In the format: ‘according to such-and-such theory’
  • Not expressing a subject’s beliefs about a domain of Fs.
  • Express a subject’s beliefs about the content of a theory purportedly about Fs.
    • Such a theory is a fiction, and thus entities postulated are fictitious entities
    • C (seminar discussion): Fictional entities
      • Q: The existence of a fictional entity is considered how?
      • Existence is often claimed to be univocal regardless of the domain it is used in. However, this is a claim supporting anti-realism because a realist would disagree; existence of fictional entity != existence of a real entity as the latter would have a formal reality according to a robust realist, whereas the former would not.
  • Prefix added to first-order logic: According to the realist’s fiction, $\exists_xFx$
  • This does not entail that $\exists_xFx$, thus there is no OCs about Fs.
  • Fiction can be useful. Realism has a certain utility.
  • Reject (C) of the Quinean theory, and challenge (P2).

29:2 29:2 30:1 30:2 30:3 2.3.3: Instrumentalism/non-cognitive fictionalism

  • POSITION: top left
  • Claims about Fs are truth-apt
    • Like error theory, such claims are false
    • But aim of the discourse is not about truth but about something else.
      • Bas van Fraassen: aim of science is simply empirical adequacy
      • Field: mathematics is valuable because it is conservative
    • Theories can be valuable without it being true and positing the existence of hypothetical entities.
  • Unlike prefix fictionalism, instrumentalism takes claims at face value. No paraphrasing is necessary.
    • Achieves this by not encouraging belief but acceptance (quasi-assertions: express one’s acceptance).
  • Reject (P1) of the Quinean argument.
    • There is a difference between assertion and quasi assertions, and beliefs and assertions.
  • Cohen: to accept p is to have or adopt a policy of deeming, positing, or postulating that p — that is, of going along with that proposition…
  • One can suppose that p without believing it
    • Supposition != acceptance.
      • Acceptance involves a belief that what is accepted is useful in some way.
      • Acceptance suggests assenting to p
      • C: purely a linguistic distinction? I would just call it different levels of acceptance. As Cohen said it: ‘either for long term or for immediate purposes only’, so that is like ‘temporary acceptance’ or ‘per-case-basis acceptance’, which is the same as a supposition
        • C: example; when I do a mathematics or physics problem that requires an assumption or approximation. I am not equating the supposed to the supposition. However, I have a level of acceptance that this acceptance is adequate for this situation. Therefore, I can say to accept my assumption is true in this case. I can also say suppose my assumption is true in this case.

30:4 31:1 31:2 2.3.4: Non-factualism/non-cognitivism

  • POSITION: top left
  • Statements about a domain do not express a subject’s beliefs about that domain.
  • Utterances within the domain are not representations of facts. They are not truth-apt
    • They do not have any possible truth-values.
  • Unlike non-cognitive fictionalists, claims should not be taken at face value, not because they contain hidden prefixes or use metaphorical devices, but because:
    • the surface grammatical form of such utterances does not reflect their logical form, for such utterances are grammatically declarative sentences
      • Declarative: express propositions.
      • Propositions: representations, truth-apt and can be believed.
      • All denied by the non-factualist. These utterances are not assertoric. Definition.
        • They are for expressing something else: like attitudes, prescribe actions, etc.
    • No distinction between acceptance and belief. For them, claims about Fs cannot be believed, and their content is not truth-apt, thus the so-called act of believing is the same as the act of accepting such utterances and moving on.
  • Takes issue with (P2) of the Quinean argument, cannot do that with (P1), since our beliefs are the same as our acceptances.
    • Utterances are not representational, therefore cannot be a part of any theory that is ontologically committed to Fs.
      • If we have to commit ourselves to Fs through these non-truth-apt utterances, then (P2) is false. Avoid OCs.

31:3 32:1 32:2 2.3.5: Hermeneutic and revolutionary conceptions of anti-realism

  • Distinction
    • Hermeneutic
      • Capture metaphysical commitments and linguistic practices
      • Aside from Error theory, anti-realist strategies give a way of construing thoughts and talk with unwanted OCs. The construals are either:
        • Reconstructed to become descriptive (not prescriptive) analyses
        • Theories of what such thought in fact amounts to.
      • Competitor to error theories.
        • However, theories outlined in this section need not be hermeneutic and can complement error theory.
    • Revolutionary
      • The analyses are prescriptive reinterpretations of discourse. Either through fictionalism, instrumentalism or non-factualism (as opposed to eliminativism).

Summary

This chapter went in-depth about why one would want to be a realist, either globally or locally, about a certain domain, likewise, why one would want to be an anti-realist. It motivates realism through IBE and Quine’s argument for minimal realism, which concerns the concept of ontological commitments. But, it motivates anti-realism by several responses through the horns of Parsimony (sui generis) or Attitude (reductive realism). It also details several anti-realist arguments, which belong to the top-left quadrant of the axis, namely the mind-independent but non-existent. Finally, it places anti-realist arguments into either the hermeneutic or revolutionary approach.

37:1 37:2 37:3 37:4 3.2 Social constructivism

  • POSITION: bottom right
  • One way an entity is to depend on us is by being socially constructed
    • The list of things that are said to be socially constructed is so large that it is hard to unify them.
  • Hacking’s description of SC:
    • Social constructionists about X tend to hold that:
    1. In the present state of affairs, X is taken for granted; X appears to be inevitable
    2. X need not have existed, or need not be at all as it is. X, or X as it is at present, is not determined by the nature of things; it is not inevitable.
    3. X is quite bad as it is.
    4. We would be much better off if X were done away with, or at least radically transformed
  • For example: those who claim that gender, race or sexual orientation are socially constructed tend to claim that there is prejudice in those domains.
    • If we choose to reject them such prejudices will disappear or diminish.
  • Although some things can be socially constructed without clause 0,2 or 3.
    • Thus, clause 1 seems like a necessary condition for something to be socially constructed.

38:1

  • What are socially constructed entities determined by, if not by the “nature of things”?

      • X is said to supervene on Y iff all things Y-indiscernible is also X-indiscernible.

42:1 42:2 42:3 3.3 Response dependence

  • POSITION: bottom right
  • Non-idealism view of mind-dependence entity
    • Appeals to traditional distinction between primary and secondary qualities.
      • Johnston, Wright, Pettit: introduce response-dependent concept
      • Response-dependent concepts: concepts whose extensions depend in a very special way on us and our mental responses.
        • A concept F is response-dependent iff there is a bi-conditional of the form “x is F iff x is disposed to produce response R in subject S in conditions C” that is knowable a priori
      • A lot of properties belong to the RDC. Not all of them are secondary qualities.

42:4 43:1

  • Utmost important that the truth of the bi-conditional is knowable a priori, since any concept may coincidentally provoke a response in subjects like us.
    • Example: mistaking mass to be an RDC, but not justifying that there is an a priori connection between the concept and the judgement.
    • Example: glass is fragile if they and only if they are disposed to shatter when dropped
      • Fragility is not an RDC; the subject has to be humans or persons, and they must produce a response that is a mental process, an affective, conative or cognitive state. (Conation = volition or desire)
  • An interesting RDP: a judgement-dependent concept; concept whose extensions are determined by our judgements or beliefs employing that concept.

43:2 44:1

  • Final point for RDC
    • the conditions specified identify restrictions on the subjects and environment required for it to be knowable a priori.
      • It is not a priori that something is red iff it causes red sensations in humans like us.
        • Unconscious or blind people will not have this kind of experience, nor will normal-sighted people when it is covered in a blanket or in a dark room.
    • C-conditions state explicitly the required subject and environment.
  • Wright: for every concept there is a basic equation associated with it that is knowable a priori.
    • Example: for a concept F, the following bi-conditionals are a priori knowable:
      • x is F iff
        • an omniscient God would judge that x is F
        • subjects who are good at identifying Fs would believe that x is F in favourable environments.
        • Etc.
    • C-conditions of an RDC must always be NON-TRIVIAL.

44:2

  • RDCs pick out dispositional properties, and dispositional properties are intrinsic.
    • Thus, they do not metaphysically nor causally depend on us.
    • The potential of some entity to have the property F-ness does not depend on our physical and mental existence
      • Example: addictiveness of tobacco.

44:3

  • This distinction that RDCs are intrinsic undermines anti-realism, because intrinsic properties seem inappropriate in an anti-realist’s bag of tools.
  • This fact is relieved in support of anti-realism through three compromises (Pettit 1991):
    • (i) RDCs implicate the subject in a way that RICs do not. There are non-empty concepts essentially connected to our mental states. It would be uncomfortable to suggest that objective reality behave in such way to be so connected to our mental world. Thus, this is a good reason to think that RDCs are in conflict with realism.
    • (ii) Extensions of RDCs do not meet the constraint of ‘ontic neutrality’. There is no reason to think that other species beside humans will have the same responses. This furthers support for anti-realism.
    • (iii) (The insecurity thesis)[#61] is in conflict with RDCs. RDCs in fact cannot lead subjects astray. This is certainly true for many examples.

44:4

  • There is a reason against RDCs being anti-realist. Wright considered this worry:
    • Consider the case of intention: It is a priori that x intends to $\psi$ iff x is disposed to believe that she intends to $\psi$ in situations where she is not self-deceived. Thus, intention is RDCs, then our mental states are in part not mind-dependent.
      • Q: Is this in part reductio ad absurdum?

46:1

  • Wright suggests perhaps mental concepts are not RDCs
    • In situations where she is not self-deceived is a C-condition that is non-trivial and hence makes it a priori, however, what does this C-condition mean?
      • It must presume she is not self-deceived when she knows she is not self-deceived.
      • This is tautological and is thus trivial.
      • This condition is trivial and thus intentions are not a form of RDC, keep our mental states separate from RDCs.

46: 2

  • Any attempt to undermine realism using mind-dependence appears preposterous or empty
    • Preposterous: idealism and verificationism
  • Two good attempts are Social Constructivism (SC) and Response-dependence concepts (RDCs)

Summary

This chapter talks about the dependence axis. It mentions a few theories in the top left quadrant, such as error theory, fictionalism, instrumentalism, non-factualism, and a few theories in the bottom right quadrant, such as social constructivism and response-dependent theory. It discusses the possibility of these theories and how they might or might not be successful at defending anti-realism or critiquing realism. They want to challenge the quiestist’s claim that realism and anti-realism eventually breaks down into the same thing. By showing that mind-dependence entities are not a member of the robust realist’s world, then they can defeat this. By doing this, they also achieve some direct opposition to realist theories where the domains can invoke opposing views.

    1. When science uses mathematical theories

      • Euler’s formula
      • Hardy-Weinberg law that uses the binomial theorem to justify a law of genetics.

  • Psychological intuitionism
    • POSITION: bottom right
    • Brouwer: mathematics is an activity of the mind.
      • The objects of mathematics are mental constructs.
      • The properties of these objects are those that can be proved of them
        • Proved entities and statements altogether make up the theory used to prove it.

168:2

  • Simple methods can be proved in a sequence of simple steps
  • Complex methods follow a set of canons of intuitionist proofs, see BHK intepretation

168:3

  • Intuitionist interpretation of logical particles contrasted against classical interpretation
    • Example:
      • Law of the excluded middle can fail to be true
        • $p$ : $A \lor \lnot A$ is always true
      • $p$ implies a tautology in classical logic
        • But BHK denies this: we need a proof of A for the statement to be a tautology.
        • For there to be A, we need something that has the property A (or to find that nothing has the property A).
          • This is a demanding task, thus for a given A where we cannot prove, the law of the excluded middle fails to be true.

169:1

  • Virtues of psychological intuitionism
    1. Ontological economy: does not postulate anything outside the mind.
    2. Epistemological: we can have knowledge of mathematics and its object by reflecting on the operation of the human mind. C: It also obviously solves the access problem

169:2

  • Mental construction
    • Mathematical process is an activity not in language.
    • Adopt Kantian intuition of time for numbers.

169:3

  • Psychologizes mathematics is an unattractive view for scientific realists
    • Are theories about sub-atomic particles about our minds instead?
    • Scientific laws are thus laws about the workings of human minds.

169:4

  • Non-psychological intuitionism
    • POSITION: top left
    • Dummett: intuitionism about mathematics is a form of verificationism
    • Like structuralists, there are no special sets
    • We can perform a step/procedure that takes us to the next term in the sequence (each iteration is like a computer program’s iteration), in a systematic manner.
      • All we need is a description of the procedure
        • C: Below is my try at an example
        • Example: take the next increment in time and compare it to the previous increment in time and see how our physical entity’s properties have changed. Imagine these increments are so very tiny, then we can describe this iterative change more accurately.
      • C: At first glance, this seems to work for most fields in mathematics. Have a think later…

170:1

  • Intuitionist constructions are merely computational instructions
  • Thus, they are mind-independent entities, but they are not physically real.

170:2

  • A mathematical object exists iff there is a procedure that can prove its existence.

170:3

  • Infinite theories can be have endless procedures.
  • They have potential infinity
    • Contrasted with actual or complete infinity.
    • Complete sets have all members at one time
    • Potential set at any time is incomplete
    • There are no complete sets, only potential sets
      • C: this is quite a naturalist approach

170:4

  • It is still anti-realist
    • Capability of human reasoning establishes boundary for what can count as a mathematical object.
    • Rejects vast majority of objects postulated by standard mathematics (Example: transfinite sets)

171:1

  • Virtues of non-psychological intuitionism
    • Solves the access problem
    • Avoids the epistemological problem because have knowledge once we can produce them from our constructed steps.
    • Does not commit to any mathematical abstraction, only intuitive steps.

171:2

  • Intuitionism (both branch) is revisionary, a revolutionary form of anti-realism (as opposed to hermeneutics)
    • Does not claim to capture all of mathematics.
    • Does not claim to explain common practices.
    • Claims that much of ordinary mathematics is epistemologically suspect/meaningless
      • Proofs for property without finding out if an object can inherit it.
      • We need a means for finding out an example of X to prove a set of statements about X

171:3

  • Some numerical methods, such as the intermediate value theorem cannot seem to be proven using intuitionist mathematics. Since the point we want cannot be found analytically, only in relation to other points. Cannot logically deduce from other points the existence of the desired value.
  • Hence, it is unpopular

172:1

  • Intuitionism thinks their theory provides all the necessary mathematics.
    • All scientific applications included
    • Challenged by Hellman (1993)
      • Cannot be used in quantum mechanics, since there are postulates which we may not have a direct proof of.
    • Response:
      1. Intuitionist mathematics can come up with different but nevertheless empirically adequate theories
      2. Admit more work is needed to construct new structures for modern physics.

172:2

  • Trade-off: clear epistemology in return for incomplete coverage of classical mathematics.

172:3 10.9 Response-dependence

  • POSITION: bottom right
  • Kitcher: attempts to justify all of classical mathematics
    • Mathematics is an idealisation of our mathematical abilities.
    • C: what I understand from this short segment:
      • Everything we develop is attributing to the ideal mathematician, we are merely responding to the statements from this god-like mathematician, this response corresponds to understanding.

173:1

  • This avoids the epistemological and metaphysical problems that plague Platonism.
  • But has its own problems:
    • Metaphysical: Can there exist a god-like mathematician? What sort of world do they exist in? Are they fictional?
    • Epistemological: Can we understand as a response to this ideal mathematician? Do we know what it is like to be this mathematician? How are we able to access their understanding as our minds apprehend mathematics? It is not a 1:1 mapping of our cognitive ability
      • Example: they can have complete infinite sets, we can only have potential ones.

Summary

A taxonomy of realist and anti-realist theories about how mathematical theories and objects have their place in our world. Each theory is evaluated on its efforts to tackle the epistemological (Access problem) and metaphysical (specifically: ontological).
